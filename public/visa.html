Can you use Japan's Digital Nomad status?

Use this page as a first-pass eligibility check. It is not legal advice, and visa rules can change. Before applying, confirm the latest requirements with Japan's Immigration Services Agency, the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, or a qualified immigration professional.

Quick answer

Japan's Digital Nomad status is designed for high-income remote workers who will stay in Japan for no more than six months while working for an overseas employer or overseas clients. It is not a route for taking a job with a Japanese company, building a Japan-based client pipeline, or settling long term.

Step 1

Check the core requirements

You may fit if

You work remotely for overseas income

Your employer is outside Japan, or your clients/customers are outside Japan, and the work can be delivered online without needing to be physically in Japan.

You may fit if

Your stay is six months or less

The Digital Nomad status allows a stay of six months. It cannot be renewed, although the official guidance says a new stay may be possible after leaving Japan for six months.

You may fit if

Your income is at least JPY 10 million

The applicant must personally have annual income of at least JPY 10 million at the time of application.

You may fit if

You have qualifying medical insurance

Your medical insurance must cover the planned stay and include at least JPY 10 million in treatment expense coverage for injury and illness.

Step 2

Understand what work is allowed

Usually aligned with the status

  • Working online for a foreign employer while temporarily staying in Japan.
  • Providing paid services online to clients located outside Japan.
  • Selling goods or services online to overseas customers, where the activity does not require entering Japan.
  • Combining tourism, community events, and remote work as long as the work stays within the permitted scope.

High-risk or outside the scope

  • Employment under a contract with a Japanese public or private organization.
  • Work that can only be provided or sold because you entered Japan.
  • Starting to serve Japanese clients while in Japan without checking the immigration and tax consequences.
  • Using short-term visitor status as if it were a work status.

Step 3

Prepare the document story

Think of the application as a story you need to prove: who pays you, where that payer is located, what you will do from Japan, how long you will stay, how you meet the income threshold, and how your medical costs are covered.

  • Identity and application basics: passport and visa application materials.
  • Activity plan: explanation of your planned stay, remote work, and period in Japan.
  • Income evidence: documents showing annual income of at least JPY 10 million.
  • Insurance evidence: proof of medical insurance covering the stay, with at least JPY 10 million treatment expense coverage.
  • Work relationship evidence: employment contract, client contracts, or other proof that your work is for overseas parties.
  • Nationality or region check: confirm that your country or region is included in the official target list.

Step 4

Plan for practical friction after arrival

No residence card

This status is not eligible for a residence card. That can make address proof, housing screening, bank accounts, phone contracts, and some local procedures harder.

No renewal

Design the trip around a real six-month ceiling. If your plan depends on staying longer, this status is probably the wrong tool.

Tax and labor questions

Long or repeated stays, family accompaniment, Japan-facing clients, or employer-directed work in Japan can create tax, labor, social insurance, or permanent establishment questions.

Tax planning

Your visa status is not your tax answer

Check tax residence, Japan-source income, and treaty relief separately

The Digital Nomad status permits a temporary remote-work stay, but it does not create a blanket tax exemption. Work performed while physically in Japan can still raise Japanese tax questions, and the 183-day rule is usually a treaty issue rather than Japan's basic residence test.

Decision guide

Which path sounds closest?

Mostly tourism, checking email occasionally

Look at short-term visitor rules first. Do not assume paid work is allowed just because the stay is short.

Remote work for overseas employer or clients for up to six months

The Digital Nomad status may be worth checking if you meet the income, insurance, nationality or region, and activity requirements.

Working for a Japanese company or Japanese clients

This guide is probably not the right route. You should check ordinary work statuses or speak to an immigration professional.

Staying longer than six months or relocating

Treat this as a longer-term immigration, tax, housing, and employment planning question rather than a digital nomad stay.
