1 Bank Accounts

Bank Accounts

A digital nomad status does not automatically mean you can open a Japanese bank account. Banks apply their own screening and identity checks, so the answer may depend on your residence status, period of stay, address evidence, identity documents, tax or compliance information, and the bank's risk controls.

Many digital nomads never need a Japanese bank account.

For a short stay, it is usually safer to plan around foreign cards, foreign-account cash withdrawals, and emergency backup cards rather than assuming a local account will be available immediately.

2 Credit Cards

Credit Cards

Prefer broad acceptance

Bring a Visa card and a Mastercard if possible. Do not build your entire payment plan around a card that only works on a narrower network or a single issuer's overseas policy.

Best Practice

Carry at least two cards before you travel, ideally from different banks or networks, and keep one separate from your wallet.

5 Foreign Card Friendly ATMs

Foreign Card Friendly ATMs

Recommended places to check first

7-Eleven ATM

Japan Post Bank ATM

FamilyMart, when available

These are practical options for visitors using cards issued overseas. Many locations are open long hours, and convenience-store ATMs are often available 24 hours, but card acceptance, maintenance windows, limits, and fees can vary.

6 International Money Transfers

International Money Transfers

Many nomads keep income and savings overseas, then move living money as needed. Compare speed, fees, exchange rates, limits, identity checks, and support before relying on any route.

Bank Transfer

Pros: familiar, bank-to-bank records, suited to larger transfers.

Cons: can be slow, fees and exchange-rate spreads may be hard to predict.

Wise

Pros: transparent fee display and multi-currency tools.

Cons: availability, limits, verification, and recipient options vary.

Revolut

Pros: app-based currency exchange and card spending tools.

Cons: features, limits, and support depend on your home country and plan.

7 Apple Pay & Google Pay

Apple Pay & Google Pay

Mobile wallet acceptance continues to improve at convenience stores, transit-adjacent retailers, major chains, and many urban restaurants. Whether your card works through Apple Pay or Google Pay depends on your card issuer, card network, device region, and wallet configuration, so test it before departure and keep the physical card with you.

8 Japanese QR Payments

Japanese QR Payments

Japan has many QR payment brands, including PayPay, Rakuten Pay, and d払い. They are useful for residents, campaigns, and domestic app ecosystems, but short-stay foreign visitors may face different registration, phone number, identity, bank account, or card-linking requirements. For most digital nomads, overseas card payments plus cash are the more common baseline.

9 Before You Fly Checklist

Before You Fly Checklist

  • Bring two bank cards
  • Notify your bank
  • Enable overseas transactions
  • Prepare some Japanese yen
  • Know your ATM PIN
  • Register Apple Pay or Google Pay
  • Save emergency card numbers

FAQ

Common payment questions

Can I rely only on cash?

You can use cash widely, but relying only on cash makes hotel deposits, online bookings, train apps, and emergencies harder.

Should I use JCB?

JCB is common in Japan, but visitors should not depend on JCB only. A Visa plus Mastercard setup is usually more resilient.

Are ATM fees always the same?

No. Fees can depend on the ATM operator, card network, issuer, time, account type, and currency conversion choice.
